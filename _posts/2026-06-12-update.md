# Data Types / What is a pattern?

We say that patterns encode garments, but this does not mean that there is a straighforward correspondence <sup>a bijective map</sup> between one garment and one pattern. A single pattern often provides instructions for making multiple garments. But it's not enough to consider a one-to-many map from patterns to garments because the archives we work with often contain duplicates of each pattern. This might not be a problem if each copy were in perfect condition so that we could treat them all as exchangeable, but because each pattern is worn or cut or folded in a different way, and because our scanning methods are imperfect, we have to think about each physical pattern as a different object. So, a single pattern can encode multiple garments, and a single garment may be represented multiple times in the archive. To address these problems, we spent some time defining our data types. We think these types are more interesting than just an artifact of our code.

## Archival World

In the *archival world*, we have objects that are attached to physical things in the real world, either at the [Commercial Pattern Archive](https://copa.apps.uri.edu) (CoPA) or in our scanning set-ups.

### `Pattern`'s

`Pattern` objects are the physical patterns that are held by CoPA. CoPA has 61,000 patterns, each of which lives in in a file cabinet in the special collections of the University of Rhode Island in Kingston, RI.

![pattern grid]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/pattern_grid.png)

A `Pattern` consists of an envelope with the manufacturer and an ID number, an illustration of the assembled garments the pattern describes, tissue paper sheets with full size pattern panels printed on it, extensive and variable annotations on the panels, textual assembly instructions, miniature diagrams of the pattern panels, and detailed sewing tips. Here's a collage of what some of this information looks like.

![pattern collage]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/pattern_collage.png)

`Patterns` can also include other, highly heterogeneous data, like the following invitation to write to Meg Carter on Park Avenue for sewing tips.

![sew along]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/sew_along.png)

### `Scan`'s

A `Scan` object is the set of images we produce when we scan a pattern. Each image is depicts pattern pieces, instruction sheets, or pattern covers. An image might contain multiple of these components, which happens when we image multiple pieces of paper at the same time for efficiency. An example of a `Scan` is below (but a full scan would also include images of things like the instructions).

![scan sheets]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/scan_sheets.png)

Each `Scan` corresponds to a unique `Pattern`. The same `Pattern` could have multiple `Scan`'s, for example if we wanted to re-image the same pattern to improve image quality.

## Digital World

In the *digital world*, objects are mathematical representations of the things we extract from our images of the objects in the archival world. Digital objects here come from objects in the archival world, but they are no longer attached to specific archival objects. Ideally, they are abstracted representations of the information that the archival objects were originally trying to encode.

### `Panel`'s

A `Panel` is an individual pattern panel, cleaned and extracted from the `Scan` that depicts it. Importantly, a `Panel` is no longer attached to the specific image it came from. It is stored as a vector graphic showing the edges of the panel. The image below shows a `Panel`, rendered on top of the `Scan` image it came from.

![panel example]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/panel_example.png)

`Panel`'s can also contain annotations, which can be in the form of text or markings. These annotations can either be associated with a specific line or edge on the panel, or with the panel in general.

### Other things in the digital world

We will need to extract and represent other digital objects. In particular, we will need to figure out which panels go into each garment, and how they fit together. For example, diagrams like this of the panels that go into a specific garment will be helpful.

![minimap]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/minimap.png)

Textual instructions will also help us in determining how panels fit together to form garments (just like they do for people sewing the garments at home), and we will need to extract and represent instructions in the digital world too.

## Garment World

In the *garment world*, we assemble the objects in the digital world into the garments that we were always trying to represent. To illustrate the difference between the digital world and the garment world, we emphasize that the challenge in the digital world is only in accurately extracting information from the objects in the archival world. The challenge in the garment world is in combining the information from the digital world to accurately build a garment. Nowhere in the archival world is a garment directly represented, so this is a distinct challenge from simply extracting information.

### `Garment`'s

A `Garment` is a list of the `Panel`'s that are used in a garment, as well as correspondences between each panel edge that is sewn together to form the garment. Identifying the `Panel`'s that are part of a `Garment` and the edge correspondences is a technical step we have not solved yet, but we describe our work on it below!

The garment world is the end of the pipeline for us. Once we have a list of panels that go into a garment and a list of edges that correspond to each other, we can upload the panels and these correspondences to a rendering application like Clo to show the garment.

# Technics

Separately from the problem of defining types and the architecture of our pipeline is the problem of actually moving from type to type. Abstracting these moves in this way makes our work modular; if someone on our team wants to work on better panel extraction, they can write new code to do that and swap it in to the larger process without rewriting anything else. In this side, we describe some progress on the technical processes of moving between our data types.

## Scanning

We have two set-ups for scanning. The overhead camera set-up consists of a mirrorless digital camera mounted on a pole that photographs pattern pieces lying on the ground underneath a clear acrylic slide. 

![camera]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/camera.png)

The second set-up is a roller scanner designed for imaging large flat documents. 

<video src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/pattern-magic/pattern-magic.github.io/master/images/2026-06-12-update/roller.MOV"></video>

We've found that it is important to pass the pattern through the scanner in a plastic sleeve to prevent the rollers from wrinkling the delicate tissue paper.

![wrinkles]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/wrinkles.png)

So far, it appears that both scanning methods produce images of high enough quality that we can extract the information we need to populate the digital world. As we work towards scanning hundreds or thousands of patterns, we are considering which scanning methods are most time efficient and easy to execute. It would also be exciting to develop scanning methods that people at home can emulate with few additional supplies, allowing people to digitize their own pattern collections.

## Panel Extraction

Using basic image processing techniques, we are processing `Scan` images and estimating the shape of the `Panel`'s in the image. We begin by cleaning the raw images to remove background and sharpen lines. Then we identify the closed shapes that represent panels. Finally, we convert these shapes into vectorized representations of the lines on the edges of the panels.

![panel extraction]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/panel_extraction.png)

A challenge in forming the vectorized line representation is identifying corners where edges meet, and distinguishing those from bends in the same edge. In principle, we can handle misidentifications of the corners because the edge correspondences can be many-to-many (so, if an edge is erroneously split into two lines, both of those lines can simply have the same correspondences). But this does make the edge correspondence problem harder, so we are working on the vectorization approach to accurately separate the sides that our human eyes can understand as different edges.

We are also working on identifying textual annotations on the panel. We have found a rich literature in the world of map reading. We are trying to use [MapReader]{https://joss.theoj.org/papers/10.21105/joss.06434} to find text on our panels.

![mapreader]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-06-12-update/mapreader.png)

The text identification is far from perfect, but we are hoping to keep tuning it to identify more textual annotations. We also hope MapReader might be useful for things other than text identification because the central problem in reading maps -- extracting lines -- is similar to the problem of reading patterns.

## Edge Correspondences

Our major unsolved technical challenge is how to identify which edges are stitched together to form each garment. We are drawing on various previous contributions to do this.

A 2013 [paper](https://www.floraine.org/research/clopat/) of Floraine Berthouzoz, Akash Garg, Danny Kaufman, Eitan Grinspun, and Maneesh Agrawala uses heuristics including edge lengths, edge curvatures, panel names, and annotations to estimate the probability that a pair of edges are stitched together. To replicate their method, we first will need to improve our method of reading panel names and line annotations. 

More recently, [AutoSew](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.22052) by Pablo Ríos-Navarro, Elena Garces, and Jorge Lopez-Moreno provides a big-model approach to finding edge correspondences. They train a neural network to form these correspondences, but a major challenge to using their methods in our setting is the messiness of our data. AutoSew is trained to work on synthetic patterns with perfectly clean representations of panels and edges. In contrast, we know that our reconstructed `Panel`'s are messy. On the other hand, we also have access to more information in the form of annotations and instructions. We are thinking of ways to adapting methods developed in AutoSew to our setting, both to handle our messier data and to leverage our additional information.
